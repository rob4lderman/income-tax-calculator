Income [help]

Adjusted Gross Income includes not just wages from your job (W2) but any other source of income, for example:

  • Interest on your bank account (1099-INT)
  • Dividends on stock (1099-DIV)

Learn more at irs.gov.

Income
Medicare Wages (W-2 box 5) [help]

W2 box 5: Medicare wages and tips typically includes all income earned from employment wages (medicare taxes apply to all earned wages).

This includes wages spent on tax-deferred contributions to a retirement account (e.g. a 401-K).

Wages (W-2 box 1) [help]

W2 box 1: Wages, tips, other comp. typically includes all income earned from employment wages after deducting tax-deferred contributions to a retirement account (e.g. a 401-K).

Tax-deferred contributions to a retirement account are typically listed in W-2 box 12.

Interest Income (1099-INT) [help]

Interest income, e.g. earned on a savings account. Your financial institution should provide you with a 1099-INT form indicating the amount of interest earned.

Ordinary Dividends (1099-DIV) [help]

Income from ordinary dividends, e.g. from stock ownership. Your financial institution should provide you with a 1099-DIV form indicating the amount of dividends earned.

Dividends fall into two categories:

  • Ordinary dividends: Taxed the same as ordinary income. Most dividends are ordinary dividends.
  • Qualified dividends: Taxed at a lower rate, closer to the capital gains tax rate.

Learn more at irs.gov

Adjusted Gross Income [help]

Adjusted Gross Income is the sum of all income (including wages, interest, dividends, etc) after deducting tax-deferred contributions to a retirement account (e.g. a 401-K).

Deductions [help]

Deductions are subtracted from your income and therefore reduce the amount of income that is taxed.

You can choose either the standard deduction or itemized deductions. You can't do both. So if the total value of your itemized deductions does not exceed the standard deduction, don't bother itemizing.

Learn more at irs.gov.

Itemized Deductions [help]

Common itemized deductions include:

  • Mortgage Interest
  • Student Loan Interest
  • Charitable Contributions
  • State Income Tax or State Sales Tax (can't do both)
  • Large Medical Expenses

Learn more at irs.gov.

Tax Credits[help]

Tax Credits are subtracted from the amount of tax you owe.

Credits are either refundable (meaning you get the full credit even if it amounts to more than the tax you owe) or non-refundable (meaning you only get credit up to the amount of tax you owe).

Common tax credits include:

  • Earned Income Tax Credit
  • Premium Tax Credit (Obamacare)
  • Child Tax Credit

Learn more at irs.gov.
