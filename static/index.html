Income [?]

Income includes not just wages from your job (W-2) but all other sources of income, for example:

  • Interest on your bank account (1099-INT)
  • Dividends on stock (1099-DIV)

Learn more at irs.gov.

Wages [?]

W-2 box 1: Wages, tips, other comp. typically includes all income earned from employment wages after deducting tax-deferred contributions to a retirement account (e.g. a 401-K).

Tax-deferred contributions to a retirement account are typically listed in W-2 box 12.

Social Security Wages [?]

W-2 box 3: Social security wages and tips typically includes all income earned from employment wages, up to a maximum limit of {{ TaxRates.socialsecurity.single.top | currency:"$":0 }}.

This includes wages spent on tax-deferred contributions to a retirement account (e.g. a 401-K).

The income limit is referred to as the benefit base. It's adjusted annually.

Medicare Wages [?]

W-2 box 5: Medicare wages and tips typically includes all income earned from employment wages.

This includes wages spent on tax-deferred contributions to a retirement account (e.g. a 401-K).

Interest Income [?]

1099-INT: Interest income, e.g. earned on a savings account. Your financial institution should provide you with a 1099-INT form indicating the amount of interest earned.

Ordinary Dividends [?]

1099-DIV: Dividend income: Income from ordinary dividends, e.g. from stock ownership. Your financial institution should provide you with a 1099-DIV form indicating the amount of dividends earned.

Dividends fall into two categories:

  • Ordinary dividends: Taxed the same as ordinary income. Most dividends are ordinary dividends.
  • Qualified dividends: Taxed at a lower rate, closer to the capital gains tax rate.

Learn more at irs.gov

Deductions [?]

Deductions are subtracted from your income and therefore reduce the amount of income that is taxed.

Personal Exemption [?]

The personal exemption is deducted from your taxable income.

In general you can claim one personal exemption each for yourself, your spouse, and any dependents.

Learn more at irs.gov.

Standard Deduction [?]

You can choose either the standard deduction or itemized deductions. You can't do both.

Choose whichever is larger, so as to reduce your taxable income as much as possible.

Learn more at irs.gov.

Itemized Deductions [?]

Common itemized deductions include:

  • Mortgage Interest
  • Student Loan Interest
  • Charitable Contributions
  • State Income Tax or State Sales Tax (can't do both)
  • Large Medical Expenses

Learn more at irs.gov.

State Tax [?]

You can deduct either state income tax or state sales tax, but not both.

W-2 box 17: State income tax indicates the amount of state tax you've paid.

Note: If you choose to deduct state income tax, and you end up getting a refund, you have to claim the refund as income on your federal income taxes next year.

Mortgage Interest [?]

Interest paid on a mortgage you own.

Other Deductions [?]

Tax Credits[?]

Tax Credits are subtracted from the amount of tax you owe.

Credits are either refundable (meaning you get the full credit even if it amounts to more than the tax you owe) or non-refundable (meaning you only get credit up to the amount of tax you owe).

Common tax credits include:

  • Earned Income Tax Credit
  • Premium Tax Credit (Obamacare)
  • Child Tax Credit

Learn more at irs.gov.
